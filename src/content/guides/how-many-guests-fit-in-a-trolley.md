Transportation is one of the last things most couples plan and one of the first things guests notice. Get the sizing right and nobody thinks about it twice — everyone climbs aboard, the doors close, and the day keeps moving. Get it wrong, and you're either paying for empty seats or leaving half the wedding party standing on a curb. Here's how to figure out exactly what you need before you request a quote.

## Know Your Head Count Before You Shop

Before comparing vehicles, separate your transportation needs into distinct groups, because they rarely travel together:

- **The wedding party** — you, your partner, the bridal party, and immediate family who need to be at the ceremony site early and the reception on time.
- **Out-of-town guests** — typically shuttled from a hotel block to the ceremony and reception.
- **The full guest list** — relevant if you're offering a shuttle from the reception venue back to a hotel at the end of the night, especially if alcohol is being served.

Most couples only need to solve for one or two of these groups, not all three. A 30-person wedding party ride is a very different vehicle than a 150-guest hotel shuttle running multiple loops.

## Capacity at a Glance: Trolley vs. Coach Bus vs. Limo Van

| Vehicle | Typical Capacity | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Limo Van | Up to 14 passengers | Small parties, immediate family, executive airport runs |
| Classic White Trolley | 30–36 passengers | Wedding party, ceremony-to-reception transfers, photo stops |
| Super Coach Bus | Up to 57 passengers | Full guest shuttles, hotel pickups, out-of-town groups |

A [Classic White Trolley](../../fleet/classic-white-trolley/) comfortably seats a full bridal party plus parents and grandparents, with room left over for the two of you to make an entrance. Its circular perimeter seating means everyone rides facing each other — which sounds like a small detail until you're the one trying to get a candid photo of your whole crew laughing together between stops.

Once the guest list climbs past 40 or 50 people, a single trolley starts to feel tight, and that's usually the point where couples add a [Super Coach Bus](../../fleet/super-coach-bus/) to the plan — one vehicle for the wedding party's photo-friendly ride, and one larger coach handling the guest shuttle in parallel. For smaller, more intimate arrangements — a small immediate-family ride, or an executive airport transfer for out-of-town parents — a [Limo Van](../../fleet/limo-vans/) is often the more efficient (and more affordable) choice.

## How to Estimate Your Guest Count Needs

A few rules of thumb that hold up across most weddings we work on:

1. **Count RSVPs, not invitations.** Invitation lists are always bigger than the people who actually show up and need a ride. Wait until RSVPs are in before locking a vehicle size.
2. **Subtract the locals.** Guests driving in from twenty minutes away rarely need a shuttle. Your real shuttle count is usually your out-of-town block plus anyone without a car at the venue.
3. **Add 10% for plus-ones and last-minute changes.** It's easier to have a couple of empty seats than to turn a guest away at the door.
4. **Ask your venue about parking.** If parking is limited or valet-only, more guests will opt into the shuttle than you'd expect — plan capacity accordingly.

## Splitting Wedding Party vs. Guest Shuttles

One of the most common mistakes we see is couples trying to put everyone — wedding party and guests — into a single vehicle "to keep it simple." In practice, this usually means the wedding party is stuck waiting around for a bus that's also picking up guests from three different hotels.

The cleaner approach: book a smaller, photo-worthy vehicle (trolley or limo van) exclusively for the wedding party's schedule — ceremony arrival, post-ceremony photos, reception entrance — and a separate, larger vehicle for the broader guest shuttle running on its own loop. It costs more than one shared vehicle, but it removes the single biggest source of wedding-day transportation stress: waiting.

## Timing Considerations: One Big Vehicle or Multiple Trips?

If your guest count sits right on the edge between two vehicle sizes, the deciding factor is usually timing, not just seats.

- **One large vehicle, one trip** is simplest when everyone needs to arrive at roughly the same time — for example, a hotel-to-ceremony run before a fixed start time.
- **Multiple smaller trips** make sense when there's flexibility in arrival windows, or when you want the wedding party to arrive separately from guests for a grand entrance effect.
- **Return trips at night** deserve their own plan. A shuttle that ran perfectly at 4 p.m. can bottleneck at 11 p.m. if everyone tries to leave the reception in the same twenty-minute window — consider staggering return departures every 30–45 minutes rather than one mass exit.

## Getting It Right the First Time

The short version: count your real riders (not your invitation list), decide whether the wedding party and guest shuttle need to be separate vehicles, and size for your peak moment — usually the reception exit — rather than your average headcount. From there, the vehicle choice tends to make itself obvious.
